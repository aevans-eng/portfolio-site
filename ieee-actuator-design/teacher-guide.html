Companion Teacher Guide

Design Challenge: Robot Actuator

A 45-60 minute browser-based engineering design challenge for ages 15-17 — fits a single class block or splits cleanly across two periods. Students work as junior engineers, turn a robot brief into requirements, select a motor, design a gearbox, tune a controller, and defend the result in review.

Target age
15-17
Class time
45-60 minutes
Student output
Actuator design brief
Prerequisites
Basic algebra and ratio comfort
At a Glance
  • Platform: Web browser only. No accounts, installs, or backend.
  • Best use: STEM class, robotics club, enrichment block, or career-exploration session.
  • Core question: How do engineers balance force, speed, size, mass, and control in one actuator?
  • Student stance: Treated like a junior engineer, not a quiz taker.
Learning Goals

What Students Should Be Able To Explain

Translate a real-world problem into specs Turn payload, distance, and motion needs into torque, speed, size, and mass requirements.
Evaluate components under constraints Compare motors as tradeoffs, not as isolated specs.
Design under multiple simultaneous constraints Use gearing choices to trade torque, speed, packaging, efficiency, and mass.
Reason about systems See how motor choice, gearing, and control affect one another.
Interpret control behavior Read overshoot, settling, and stability as part of engineering judgment.
Defend a design Explain choices in plain English the way an engineering review demands.
Suggested Flow

Single Block (or Split Across Two Periods)

Targets 45-60 min total. A natural split point is between Stage 3 and Stage 4 — gear design wraps day one, control tuning + review opens day two.

Time Student activity Teacher look-for
7-10 min Stage 0-1: Read the brief and compute the worst-case torque requirement. Students can explain where the target number came from.
10-12 min Stage 2: Choose a motor and justify the tradeoff. They talk about constraints, not just "best" motor.
12-15 min Stage 3: Build a gearbox and iterate after visible failures. Students notice cascading effects between ratio, size, mass, and efficiency.
8-10 min Stage 4: Tune control and watch motion quality change. They use stability language instead of guessing from vibes.
8-13 min Stage 5: Review, compare, and defend the design. See the Sim-to-Real preview showing how an AI policy would behave on their actuator. Click Print Brief to produce a clean engineering handoff document with motor, gear, performance, and Forward Scope (thermal, EMC, MTBF, fault tolerance) sections. They can name the biggest risk, the next revision route, connect hardware quality to AI behavior, and identify what concept design doesn't cover.
Discussion Prompts

Questions Worth Asking Out Loud

What single decision changed the rest of your design the most?
Where did you gain performance, and what did you pay for it?
Did your control tuning expose a hardware weakness, or cover one up?
What would you change on the next iteration if a real team were waiting on you?
The Sim-to-Real preview claims an AI trained on your actuator would behave a certain way. Why might that be true? What would change if the simulation didn't match the real hardware?
If two students hit the same Stage 5 verdict with different motors and gear types, are their designs equivalent? What would it take to call one "better"?
The Print Brief includes a Forward Scope section listing thermal, EMC, MTBF, and fault-tolerance work that concept design didn't cover. Pick one of those topics and explain why a real product team can't ship without it.

Evidence of Learning

What To Collect

  • The final actuator design brief (printable from Stage 5) including the personalized Decision Trace and Forward Scope sections.
  • The student's written motor rationale and one-line "next iteration" reflection from Stage 5.
  • A short reflection on the biggest tradeoff they made.
  • A comparison between a failed iteration and the final revision.
  • An explanation of which subsystem decision created the biggest downstream effect.
Deployment Notes

Access and Inclusivity

  • Runs in a browser on laptops, desktops, Chromebooks, and tablets.
  • No account creation, downloads, plugins, or server setup required.
  • Plain-English labels are used first, with formal engineering vocabulary layered in context.
  • Students with robotics background can go deeper, while new learners can still complete a coherent run.
  • Accessibility: ARIA labels on all interactive controls; screen-reader live regions announce engineering numbers as they update; status indicators pair color with text and glyphs (no color-only signaling); 44 px touch targets on phone viewports.
  • Audio: Subtle sound cues fire on stage transitions and design verdicts. A speaker toggle in the top-right of the page mutes them entirely; preference persists per browser. Default-on at low volume.
  • Reduced motion: The Sim-to-Real preview respects prefers-reduced-motion and pauses arm animation for students who need it.
