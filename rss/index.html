Two summers ago, I scavenged materials to build a last-minute trailer hitch for a mountain biking trip. While it was functional and lasted, whenever we hit a large bump, the bike rack would swing backwards.

Instead of assuming I just had to throw more material at it, I developed a parametric MATLAB script, validated by FEA and Norton’s Machine Design, to engineer a minimalist V2 design. 

The goal: minimize the deflection using First Principles analysis without over-complicating the fabrication, and ensure it won't snap on a cross ditch.

PROJECT SUMMARY: HIGH-CLEARANCE HITCH DESIGN OPTIMIZATION

Objective: Redesign a custom trailer hitch to minimize vertical deflection using First Principles analysis (MATLAB) and FEA validation, achieving a 46% increase in torsional stiffness and a 2.7 Safety Factor under severe 4G loads."

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
The Problem: A "scrappy" V1 prototype fabricated during a 16-day co-op sprint exhibited excessive vertical deflection under dynamic loading.
The Approach: Applied First Principles to isolate the crossbar as the "softest spring" in the assembly. Developed a parametric MATLAB script using Bredt’s Thin-Wall Formula to optimize geometry for stiffness-to-weight efficiency.
The Result: A V2 design featuring an upsized 2.5" crossbar and integrated angle-iron gussets that reduced tip deflection by 31% while maintaining a 2.7 FoS under a 4G pothole load.

KEY TECHNICAL ACHIEVEMENTS
1. Mathematical Root Cause Analysis Identified that torsional twist in the crossbar was the primary driver of failure, accounting for 94% of total vertical deflection at the receiver tip.
Methodology: Used image-based scaling in SolidWorks to determine the bike rack’s Center of Gravity (COG) and resultant moment arm.
Modeling: Simplified the system into a cantilevered receiver and a non-circular shaft in torsion to maintain analytical efficiency.
2. Geometric Optimization (The "Bigger vs. Thicker" Decision) Evaluated the governing equations for the Angle of Twist to determine the most weight-efficient stiffening method.
Finding: Midline Area (Am) has a squared relationship with stiffness in the numerator, whereas wall thickness (t) has a linear relationship.
Decision: Upsized from a 2.0" to a 2.5" profile (Option A), yielding a 52.6% theoretical stiffness gain compared to V1 while maintaining cost and packaging requirements.
3. Multi-Stage Validation (MATLAB vs. FEA) Correlated hand calculations with SolidWorks Simulation to define the limits of elementary torsion theory.
Stage 1 (V1): Achieved an 8.9% variance between MATLAB and FEA, confirming the analytical model captured the primary physics.
Stage 2 (V2): Identified a 37% discrepancy caused by local wall distortion as the b/t ratio reached 10, highlighting where FEA is required to capture final geometric compliance.
4. Structural Integrity & Reinforcement Used the "Line Method" (Norton) to analyze weld shear under dynamic 4G loads.
The Finding: Analysis revealed a 0.95 Factor of Safety at the receiver-to-crossbar weld under a 720 lb (4G) load, indicating a likely failure at the upper tension face.
The Fix: Evaluated reinforcement strategies and selected an Angle Iron gusset to act as a strain relief, successfully distributing the bending moment and ensuring structural safety.
ENGINEERING RETROSPECTIVE This project demonstrates the value of First-Principles Thinking and a Bias for Action. The V1 prototype provided the empirical data necessary to anchor the V2 analysis, proving that iterative design, informed by engineering fundamentals, is the fastest path to functional results.

Part 1: The "Scrappy" Prototype

Context: To meet a strict deadline for a trip with friends, I had to act first. On evenings and weekends during my co-op I completed the build including other vehicle side projects within a 16-day sprint, with final adjustments made 24 hrs before departure.

Constraints & The "MVP" Build

The project began with a $50 budget and a tight timeline. Market options cost nearly 10 times that and significantly reduced departure angle. So I set out to fix that.

  • Fabrication Strategy: I salvaged a free donor hitch from marketplace, repurposing it to fit with the tow bar that came with the vehicle
  • The Field Test: During our 4-day trip the hitch experienced significant deflection on dynamic loads necessitating the use of ratchet straps from the bike rack to the roof rails to reduce the moment.

Part 2: The Analysis

Context: Post-trip, I wanted to figure out the root cause of the hitch deflection during the trip, not only to figure out the mistake, but also to verify the structural integrity of the design.

Modeling Strategy: Isolating the Variables

To create a manageable mathematical model, I had to determine which components contributed the most to deflection. I chose to isolate the Receiver & Crossbar assembly, treating the side plates as rigid fixed supports.

The Justification:

  • Relative Stiffness: The side plates are vertically oriented with a high Area Moment of Inertia (I) relative to the load..
  • Load Distribution: There are two side plates sharing the load, whereas the crossbar supports the entire torsional moment alone
  • The "Softest Spring" Principle: In a series mechanical system, deflection is driven by the most compliant component. 

Defining the Load Case (Image Scaling)

Lacking CAD files for the bike rack, I used an image based scaling approach to determine the Center of Gravity (COG):

  1. Scale: Imported side profile photo into SOLIDWORKS and scaled it using known wheel diameter
  2. Model: Approximated mass distribution using primitive geometries relative to the hitch pin datum
  3. Result: Calculated the resultant moment arm using the combined COG.

First Principles Modeling (MATLAB)

I wrote a MATLAB script to decouple the physics, modeling the receiver tube as a cantilever beam, and the crossbar as a non-circular shaft in torsion.

Assumptions & Simplifications: fixed beam bending of the crossbar was deemed negligible due to the dual supported nature. Torsion was identified as the critical factor due to the significant leverage from the bike rack. To approximate the angle of twist of the square crossbar, Bredt’s Thin-Wall Formula (D/t ~8) was used.

The Finding: The analysis was confirmed with torsion of the crossbar resulting in 94% of total deflection at receiver tip.

Part 3: The Solution

With the root cause identified, I used the mathematical model to optimize the V2 design.

Optimization 1 - Geometric Stiffness: To find the most efficient way to increase stiffness, I analyzed the governing equation for angle of twist.

  • Fixed Variables: Torque (T), Length (L), and Material (G) were all constrained
  • Primary Variable: The Polar Moment of Inertia (J) was the only parameter I could realistically influence.

The “Bigger vs Thicker” Decision: using Bredt’s Formula for thin-walled tubes, I compared the variables:

  • Wall Thickness (t): Has a linear relationship with stiffness
  • Midline Area (Am): Has a squared relationship.

Conclusion: Increasing the tube profile size was more cost and weight efficient than increasing the wall thickness.

The Tube Geometry Selection: I evaluated four crossbar profiles using a weighted decision matrix to determine the best choice for the application:

Optimization 2: The Weak point

While the MATLAB script optimized the stiffness , I needed to verify the strength of the critical receiver-to-crossbar joint under the dynamic loads.

The Approach (The Line Method): Following the methodology in Machine Design by Robert L. Norton, I modeled the weld as a line rather than a volumetric solid. This allowed me to integrate weld stress calculations into my MATLAB calculator to verify the order of magnitude.

  • Load Case: Weld group was analyzed under a combined load: Direct Vertical Shear plus the Moment-Induced Shear generated by the bike rack's lever arm
  • Material Specs: Assumed a standard E70 Electrode (Tensile Strength = 70,000 psi)
  • Acceptance Criteria: Per AWS D1.1, the allowable shear stress was limited to 30% of the electrode's tensile strength (~21,000 psi).

The Finding: Under the 4G dynamic load, the analysis revealed a factor of safety of 0.95 at the weld. This scenario would likely result in the receiver tube tearing away from the crossbar.

The Weld Reinforcement Selection: I evaluated three reinforcement strategies to reduce the stress concentrations at the weld.

Part 4: Verification & Results Validation

Before declaring the V2 design "safe," I needed to validate my mathematical model against the FEA results.

FEA Methodology & Setup:

To maintain accuracy while avoiding the “garbage in, garbage out” trap, I performed the following:

  • Boundary Conditions: I applied fixed geometry constraints to the end of the crossbar tubes to more closely compare the results of the MATLAB script to the FEA results
  • Remote load: Instead of modeling complex bike rack geometry, I applied a remote load at the COG calculated previously. This accurately turned the force into a normal force and moment in the receiver tube
  • Mesh Strategy: I used the standard curvature-based mesh to decrease element size around corners and features.

Stage 1: Model Validation (V1 Static): I compared my MATLAB predictions for the original V1 design against the FEA results to verify correlation between the hand calcs and simulation.

  • Load Case: 180 lbs
  • Matlab Prediction: 0.0174” vertical deflection
  • FEA Result: 0.0189” vertical deflection 

Correlation: The 8.9% variance confirmed that the analytical model was robust and captured the primary physics of the failure.

Stage 2: Defining the Limits of Hand Calculations (V2)

I repeated the validation process for the stiffened V2 design (2.5x2.5x0.25” crossbar).

  • MATLAB Prediction: 0.0087”
  • FEA Result: 0.01193”
  • The Discrepancy: Unlike the V1 model, the V2 model revealed a ~37% variance.

Conclusion: The difference can be attributed to local wall deformation in the V2 design. The width to thickness ratio increased from 8 (V1) to 10 (V2). This highlighted the limitations of the formulas used. Both Bredt’s Formula and Saint-Venant’s theory assume the cross-section remains rigid and does not deform.

Stage 3: Final Safety Check (Dynamic Load)

To validate the V2 design for real-world durability, I applied a "Worst Case" dynamic load to the full assembly, including side plates, ribs, and gusset.

Simulation Parameters:

  • Design Requirement: Torsional Safety Factor > 2.0 (Yield).
  • Load Condition: 4G Vertical Acceleration (Severe Pothole/Impact Event).
  • Applied Load: 720 lbs (Static Equivalent of 180 lbs tongue weight × 4G).

Performance Results:

  • Global Safety: The assembly achieved an average node safety factor of 2.7, significantly exceeding the 2.0 requirement,
  • Stiffness Gain: V2 yielded a 46% increase in torsional stiffness (31% reduction in deflection) compared to the V1 baseline.
  • Validation of Assumptions: Post-simulation decomposition revealed that side plate distortion contributed only ~8% to the total deflection confirming previous approximations.

FEA Interpretation & Stress Analysis:

  • Simulation Artifacts: High stress concentrations were observed at the tube-to-plate interface. These were the result of a lack of modeled weld fillets in contact gaps which would normally be filled with weld
  • Stress Concentration: A stress concentration was noted at the corner of the angle iron gusset. Under the extreme 4G load case this area experiences a reduced FoS below the requirement

Verdict: Acceptable for the project scope. Since this loading scenario represents a catastrophic event rather than a cyclical load, fatigue is a secondary consideration for the gusset.

V3 Recommendation: For future iterations, replacing the angle iron with triangular web gussets would mitigate this corner stress concentration.

Future Optimization (V3 Concept)

While the V2 design met all safety goals, further stiffness gains could be achieved without upsizing the tube by addressing two specific areas.

1. Crossbar Reinforcement ("Fish plating")

  • Concept: Weld additional steel plates to the top and rear faces of the crossbar
  • The Benefit: This increases the wall thickness locally, increasing torsional stiffness and decreasing local wall deformation
  • Why excluded from V2: The tube upsize from V1 already achieved a 46% stiffness gain, making the extra fabrication time and welding heat of fishplates unnecessary for this specific application.

2. Gusset Redesign (Triangular Ribs)

  • The Issue: The V2 angle-iron gussets rely on the corner to resist bending, which creates a stress concentration
  • The Fix: Replace the angle iron with triangular gussets
  • Expected Result: This simple geometric change would significantly reduce stress concentrations.

Conclusion: The Value of Iteration

This project bridged the gap between theoretical analysis and practical fabrication for me, reinforcing two core engineering principles:

  • First-Principles Thinking I upsized the crossbar from 2.0” to 2.5” to demonstrate how a minor geometric change yields major results (46% stiffness increase). Manually deriving this using Bredt’s Formula reinforced my intuition.
  • Bias for Action Building the "scrappy" V1 prototype broke analysis paralysis. This iterative cycle proved that imperfect action often provides better engineering data than perfect planning.

Retrospective & Next Steps: While the V2 design is verified safe for normal conditions, the simulation successfully identified local wall distortion and gusset stress concentrations as the final limit of the system. For future heavy duty applications, I would address this residual compliance by implementing the fish-plating and triangular gusset strategy discussed in the V3 concept.
