The hook

A volcanic tableland in California's far northeast is riddled with more than 700 lava-tube caves you explore by headlamp — and one of them, a natural lava fortress, is where 53 Modoc fighters held off ten times their number for months.

01Why it's worth it

Lava Beds is the most otherworldly corner of California and the least visited, because it's genuinely far. Beneath a Great Basin high-desert plain lies a labyrinth of lava tubes — over 700 known, some two dozen with marked entrances and trails so you can climb down ladders into the earth with a lantern and walk passages of frozen basalt, ice floors, and glittering mineral ceilings, entirely on your own. Above ground it holds two more distinct wonders: Captain Jack's Stronghold, the natural lava fortress where Kintpuash (Captain Jack) and his band waged the 1872–73 Modoc War, holding the US Army at bay for months in the cracked terrain — the only major Indian war fought in California, and a devastating story — and Petroglyph Point, one of the largest single panels of Native American rock art in the United States. Caves, war history, and ancient carvings in one remote monument. Nothing else in the state is like it, which is exactly why it earns a six-hour drive.

02How to experience it

It's a long haul (~6h from SF, past Shasta into the far northeast near the Oregon line); do it as an overnight based at the monument campground or Tulelake. Get free cave-entry gear guidance at the visitor center, bring your own strong headlamps and layers (caves are cold and dark), and don't skip Captain Jack's Stronghold trail or Petroglyph Point. Pairs with Mount Shasta and Burney Falls on a far-north loop.
